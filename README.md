# hackHERS
Compass – Problem-First Student Support Navigator
🌍 Overview

Students often struggle to find the right help not because resources don’t exist — but because knowing where to go is confusing, intimidating, and overwhelming.

Most support systems require students to understand institutional structures:

“Should I contact advising?”

“Is this a counseling issue?”

“Who handles academic burnout?”

In reality, students think in terms of problems, not offices.

Compass is a problem-first navigator that lets students describe what they’re experiencing in plain language and intelligently directs them toward relevant support resources.

Instead of browsing categories, students simply answer:

“What’s going on?”

💡 The Problem

Students frequently face challenges such as:

Academic stress & burnout

Mental health struggles

Financial pressure

Isolation & adjustment difficulties

Feeling overwhelmed or stuck

Despite many universities offering robust services, barriers remain:

✅ Students don’t know what resources exist
✅ Support structures are fragmented
✅ Terminology is unclear
✅ Reaching out feels intimidating
✅ Problems rarely fit neat categories

As a result, many students delay or avoid seeking help.

🚀 The Solution

Compass reimagines help-seeking as a natural conversation.

Students describe their situation in everyday language, and the system:

✅ Interprets the underlying need
✅ Identifies relevant resource types
✅ Explains why they are relevant
✅ Reduces friction to take action

Example:

User Input

“I can’t keep up with my classes and I’m extremely stressed.”

Compass Output

You may benefit from:

• Academic Advising – for workload planning & course support
• Counseling Services – for stress & emotional wellbeing
• Study Support Center – for learning strategies

Each recommendation includes:

Why it matches the situation

Suggested next steps

Low-pressure guidance

⭐ Key Design Principle

Problem-First Navigation

Traditional systems → Choose a department
Compass → Describe your experience

This mirrors how students actually think.

🧠 How It Works

Compass uses natural language understanding to map user-described problems to support domains, such as:

Academic difficulty

Emotional distress

Financial stress

Social / adjustment challenges

Urgent / crisis situations

The system focuses on guidance, not diagnosis or clinical advice.

✨ Features
🗣 Plain-Language Input

Students describe concerns conversationally — no institutional knowledge required.

🧭 Intelligent Resource Matching

AI identifies relevant support categories based on context & intent.

💬 Barrier-Aware Guidance (Unique Feature)

Compass acknowledges common hesitation and reduces psychological friction.

Example:

“Many students feel unsure about contacting advising. A simple first message could be…”

📊 Resource Confidence Indicator

Shows how strongly a resource aligns with the user’s situation.

🔄 Non-Judgmental Tone

Designed to feel supportive, not clinical or corrective.

🛠 Technical Approach

Compass is intentionally lightweight and hackathon-friendly.

Architecture

User Input → Prompted AI Interpretation → Structured Response → UI Rendering

No sensitive data storage required.
No medical claims made.
No training datasets required.

🔐 Privacy & Safety

Compass is designed with responsible AI usage in mind:

✅ No diagnosis or medical advice
✅ No collection of personal health records
✅ No tracking of sensitive user data
✅ Informational & supportive framing only

The system provides resource guidance, not treatment.

🌱 Social Impact

Compass aims to improve:

Accessibility of student support systems

Early help-seeking behavior

Mental wellbeing & academic resilience

Equity for first-gen & international students

Reduced stigma around seeking help

🎯 Intended Users

Students experiencing uncertainty, stress, overwhelm, or difficulty navigating available support systems.

🧪 Example Use Cases

Compass can assist with situations such as:

“I’m falling behind and don’t know what to do.”

“I feel isolated and can’t adjust socially.”

“Money stress is affecting my schoolwork.”

“I’m anxious about everything lately.”
